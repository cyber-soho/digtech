§ 01

Project Scenario &
Learning Goals

You are part of a junior digital‑science team in a university innovation lab that explores how AR (Augmented Reality), VR (Virtual Reality), and XR (eXtended Reality) interfaces can support future digital services in areas such as smart cities, industry, or education.

AR — Augmented Reality
Augmented Reality
Digital content overlaid on the real world in real time, often via smartphones, tablets, or smart glasses.
VR — Virtual Reality
Virtual Reality
Fully computer‑generated immersive environments typically experienced via head‑mounted displays and motion tracking.
XR — eXtended Reality
eXtended Reality
Umbrella term for AR, VR and Mixed Reality (MR), covering the full continuum from real‑to‑virtual worlds and all human–computer interactions within them.
🧠
Explain core concepts of AR, VR, and XR and their differences in your own words.
🔗
Identify at least one realistic use‑case where AR or VR extends an existing digital‑technology topic from the course (IoT, blockchain, network architecture, digital‑twin monitoring).
✏️
Sketch and describe a simple XR user interface (UI) focusing on spatial layout, interactions, and usability.
⚖️
Reflect critically on benefits, limitations, accessibility, ethics, and safety aspects of AR/VR/XR tools in real‑world‑like lab environments.
Open Lab

Any proposed tools, platforms, devices, frameworks, or techniques are only examples and can be replaced by other suitable and available alternatives. You are explicitly encouraged to research, compare, and even prototype your own tools or techniques.

Day 1
Concepts, Use‑Case & Research

3.1 Clarify Core Concepts Individual or pairs ▾

Write short, own‑words explanations (3–5 sentences each) for AR, VR, and XR. Always write the full expansion of any abbreviation in round brackets directly after first use — for example: GUI (Graphical User Interface), HMI (Human–Machine Interface).

Recommended References
  • Interaction Design Foundation — Extended Reality overview:
    interaction-design.org/literature/topics/extended-reality-xr
  • W3C (World Wide Web Consortium) — XR Accessibility User Requirements:
    w3.org/TR/xaur/
3.2 Choose a Use‑Case Linked to Course Topics 1 paragraph, 5–8 sentences ▾

Select one concrete scenario that connects AR/VR/XR to another topic from the course plan. Examples:

  • Visualizing IoT (Internet of Things) sensor data from a smart building in AR, directly on doors and devices.
  • A VR training room for explaining blockchain transaction flows or network protocols.
  • An XR dashboard to monitor Cloud‑computing resources across different data centers.

Your paragraph should address:

  • Who are the users (technicians, students, customers)?
  • What problem or need do they have?
  • Why might AR, VR, or XR be a better option than a traditional 2D screen?

§ 04

Days 2–3
XR UI Design & Interaction

4.1 Define User Tasks and Interaction Flow 3–5 key tasks ▾

Describe 3–5 key tasks a typical user should be able to perform in your XR system. Example tasks:

  • "View live sensor readings on specific machines by looking at them and selecting a floating data panel."
  • "Walk through a VR environment and trigger step‑by‑step blockchain transaction visualizations."

For each task, define the four components below:

01
Goal
What the user wants to achieve.
02
Trigger
What starts the task — menu selection, gaze, hand gesture, controller click, or voice command.
03
Main Steps
3–6 steps in simple bullet points describing the interaction sequence.
04
Feedback
How the system shows success or failure — visual highlights, sound, haptic feedback.
Design Principle

Base your decisions on recognized XR design principles: natural gesture mapping, multi‑modal input, and immediate feedback.

4.2 Sketch the Spatial User Interface No artistic talent required ▾

On paper or with a drawing tool of your choice, draw at least two simple views of your XR interface.

ViewDescriptionMust Include
Default view What the user sees when first entering the experience. Main UI elements, initial spatial layout
Task‑focused view What the user sees when a specific interaction is active. Active interaction state, feedback cues

Each sketch should include:

  • Main UI elements (menus, panels, virtual objects).
  • Approximate positions relative to the user (in front, left, right, near the floor, etc.).
  • Interaction hints (icons for hand gestures, gaze cursors, controller rays, or voice commands).
  • Short text annotations explaining what each element does and why it is placed there.
Suggested Free Tools

draw.io — browser-based diagramming, no install needed.  |  Figma — collaborative UI design tool with free tier. You are free to use any other tool or simply draw by hand.

4.3 Accessibility, Safety & Ethics W3C XAUR guidance ▾

Using resources such as W3C XR Accessibility User Requirements (XAUR), address the following:

Accessibility Risk Example Practical Mitigation
⚠ Motion Sickness Rapid camera movement or locomotion Use teleport instead of continuous movement; limit FoV during motion
⚠ Small Text UI labels too small at viewing distance Adjustable font sizes; keep text at eye‑level zones
⚠ No Audio Descriptions Visual-only feedback excludes visually impaired users Add subtitles and audio cues for all important events
Privacy & Data Protection

Discuss how you would protect user data and privacy in this XR environment. Consider: limiting data stored from motion tracking, anonymizing analytics, and complying with applicable data-protection regulations.

Ethical & Educational Context

Explain in 1–2 paragraphs how your design stays within an ethical and legal educational context and avoids encouraging unsafe or non‑authorized operations that might exist in real industrial systems. The lab is exploratory — no real‑world critical infrastructure is connected.

Integration, Reflection & Report

5.1 Connect XR to Other Course Topics At least 2 topics, 1–2 paragraphs ▾

Choose at least two topics from the overall course plan and explain how XR could support them.

Course TopicPossible XR Angle
IoT (Internet of Things) XR as a visualization layer for live sensor networks — see data floating above physical devices
Blockchain XR as a training tool to walk through transaction flows in 3D space

Where appropriate, mention relevant organizations and standards bodies and link to their official websites — for example IEEE (ieee.org), ISO (International Organization for Standardization) (iso.org).

5.2 Reflection on Tools & Creativity ≈ half a page ▾

Write a short reflective section answering these three prompts:

  • How did you select or imagine your tools and technologies?
  • Which aspects are strongly influenced by existing platforms, and which are your own innovations?
  • If you had access to more resources, how would you extend or change the design?
Open Invitation

Re‑emphasize that all tools and techniques are open choices. Invite future teams to modify, extend, replace, or completely re‑invent the system.

Suggested Structure

Academic Integrity

Use only your own formulations. Do not copy text from websites, articles, or other sources. When you use ideas, diagrams, screenshots, or definitions, cite the source and give the official link. Direct quotations must be minimal and clearly marked.

Responsible Use Statement

Clearly state in the report that all tools, platforms, and scenarios are used for educational purposes only and do not provide operational access to real industrial, financial, or critical infrastructure systems.
